# Attachment & Presentation Theory (APT)

An interpretive layer that sits on top of PRAGMA measurements to explain *why* people engage in conversations, not just *how*.


## What Is APT

APT claims that in the conversational domain and interaction domain, people can be defined/profiled across two fundamental domains: **Attachment** and **Presentation**.

PRAGMA = measurement layer. What's observable. Involvement, control, density, intent, investment patterns. No assumptions about why.

APT = interpretive layer. Uses PRAGMA data (via the Dynamics Profile and Interpretation Layer) to infer why those patterns exist.

You're not adding Attachment as a dimension alongside PRAGMA's dimensions. You're building an inference system on top of PRAGMA outputs.


## The Two Domains


### DOMAIN 1: ATTACHMENT

Why they stay. Three variables:

1. **Charm** — how much they look up to you. Status, competence, high-value signals. Makes them want your approval.

2. **Hope** — belief they can get benefit from you. Key insight: high value alone isn't enough. They need to believe you'd actually share it. Accessible high value.

3. **Fear** — consequences of losing you. Power dynamics, secrets, dependency.

Without at least one of these, no connection forms.

**Note:** Attachment applies to both sides. Asymmetry (who's chasing whom) is just Domain 1 measured on both people, then compared. It's an output, not a lever.


### DOMAIN 2: PRESENTATION

How you transmit Domain 1. Three layers:

1. **Content** — what you say. Details, information, topics.

2. **Style** — how you say it. Word choice, length, structure, timing.

3. **Expressed frame** — what investment dynamic your words imply. "I'm the selector" vs "please like me." Not internal—only matters as perceived.


### What's Not a Domain But Matters

- **Internal frame** — not a domain. A tool that makes correct expressed frame natural and sustainable.
- **Trajectory** — emerges when Domain 1 works. Not a separate lever.
- **Timing/Rhythm** — part of style within Domain 2.


## How Frame Was Resolved

Frame was debated extensively. The resolution:

Frame collapses into presentation. It's not a separate domain. It's a dimension of presentation.

Presentation has multiple layers: Content (what you say), Style (how you say it), Expressed frame (what power dynamic / investment balance your words imply). All three are just "what comes across." All external.

"Internal frame" isn't a domain at all. It's just a tool that makes good presentation easier and more consistent. A strong inner frame helps you naturally produce the right expressed frame without constant calculation.

But you could theoretically have weak inner frame and still execute perfect expressed frame through pure skill. It's just harder and less stable.


## Two Orders: Causal vs Analytical

**Causal order** (what's actually happening):

Domain 1 (Attachment) → Domain 2 (Presentation)

Their attachment drives how they present. If they're charmed, it shows in their presentation. Cause → effect.

**Analytical order** (what you can measure):

Domain 2 (Presentation) → Domain 1 (Attachment)

You observe presentation first (via PRAGMA). Then you infer attachment from patterns. You can't see attachment directly. You see behavior. Attachment is the theory explaining the behavior.

```
PRAGMA measures presentation (observable)
       ↓
Patterns accumulate over time
       ↓
Attachment inferred (theory layer)
```

Early in a conversation, you only have Domain 2 data. You don't know *why* yet. As more data accumulates, attachment inference becomes more confident.

This also means early attachment reads can be wrong. Someone might present as charmed (high interest, seeking your input) but actually be qualifying you. The pattern over time reveals the truth.


## How PRAGMA Maps to APT

### PRAGMA → Domain 2 (Presentation)

This is almost direct translation. PRAGMA measures what Domain 2 describes:

- **Content** ← Dialogic Function, Information Density, Conversational Intent
- **Style** ← Expressed Involvement, Temporal Structure, Investment
- **Expressed frame** ← Control Distribution, Investment asymmetry

Domain 2 is a simplified abstraction. PRAGMA is the granular measurement underneath it.

### PRAGMA → Domain 1 (Attachment)

This requires inference over time. Not directly visible, but deducible:

- **Charm** ← sustained Expressed Involvement, Control Distribution tilted toward you, they seek your approval (Dialogic Function: Querying, Affirming toward you)
- **Hope** ← repeated engagement on specific topics, high Expressed Involvement when discussing what you can offer, forward-looking Intent
- **Fear** ← asymmetric Control they don't challenge, careful word choices, reluctance to disengage even when disinterested

### The Hierarchy

```
PRAGMA (raw measurement, 7 dimensions)
       ↓
Dynamics Profile (composed description of segment dynamics)
       ↓
Interpretation Layer (tensions, hidden dynamics)
       ↓
Domain 2 (presentation patterns — what they're doing)
       ↓
Domain 1 (attachment inference — why they're here)
```

PRAGMA is the instrument. The Dynamics Profile composes the readings. The Interpretation Layer identifies tensions. Domain 1 and 2 are what you infer from those readings.

This gives you something actionable: measure with PRAGMA, compose with the Dynamics Profile, interpret tensions, then infer attachment dynamics through APT.


## Why This Architecture Is Smart

- PRAGMA stays clean and empirical
- Attachment interpretation can be wrong—but it's a separate layer, so errors don't contaminate your base measurements
- You can refine the inference logic independently from the dimensional analysis
- Multiple interpretive frameworks could sit on top of PRAGMA (APT is one, others could exist)


## Three Outputs from PRAGMA

PRAGMA doesn't just feed APT. It produces three parallel outputs:

| Output | Focus | What It Reveals |
|--------|-------|-----------------|
| **Behavioral Profiling** | Individual | Communication style patterns (PRAGMA direct) |
| **APT Inference** | Relational | Dynamics between specific participants |
| **APT Profiling** | Individual | Attachment bearings + presentation tendencies (aggregated from APT observations) |


### 1. Behavioral Profiling

Individual communication patterns derived directly from PRAGMA, without APT interpretation.

The distinction:

- Fact-based profiling: "This person is a developer in Berlin who likes AI."
- Behavioral profiling: "This person consistently operates from high investment, asks qualifying questions, maintains symmetric control, escalates involvement slowly."

The second is about how they interact, not who they are. It's relational and dynamic, not static.

"Relative position" is key: the same person might show up differently with different people. Their behavioral profile with you isn't their universal profile. It's shaped by the dynamic between you. So you're not profiling people. You're profiling interaction patterns between specific participants.


### 2. APT Inference

Relational dynamics between a specific participant pair. What's happening between Person A and Person B right now.

Observable (PRAGMA): repeated queries on same topic, high expressed involvement, sustained investment over time.

Inference (Attachment): charm (they admire your success) + hope (they believe you can help).


### 3. APT Profiling

Individual attachment bearings and presentation tendencies, aggregated from multiple APT Inference observations across relationships.

APT Inference = relational, specific pair ("Person A is charmed by Person B because B has money")

APT Profiling = individual, aggregated across relationships ("Person A tends to be charmed by wealth—this is their attachment bearing")

The relational observation feeds the individual profile. If you see someone get charmed by money in one relationship, charmed by status in another, charmed by expertise in another—you're learning what triggers their attachment as a person.

APT Profiling covers both domains:

| Domain | What It Reveals |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Attachment Bearings** | What triggers their attachment—what charms them, what they hope for, what they fear |
| **Presentation Tendencies** | How they present under different conditions—when they're confident vs insecure, how their expressed frame shifts, when they over-explain, when they hold back |

Examples:

- "This person gets charmed by expertise, hopes for mentorship, fears rejection" (Attachment)
- "This person's frame collapses when challenged, over-explains when insecure, goes terse when confident" (Presentation)

Behavioral Profiling tells you how someone talks. APT Profiling tells you what moves them—their vulnerabilities, motivations, levers.

APT Profiling is built from aggregated APT Inference observations across multiple relationships.

## Comparing the Three Outputs

| Aspect | Behavioral Profiling | APT Inference | APT Profiling |
|--------|---------------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Focus** | Individual | Relational | Individual |
| **Question** | "How does this person communicate?" | "What's happening between these people?" | "What moves this person?" |
| **Context** | Aggregated across conversations | Specific to participant pair | Aggregated across relationships |
| **Output** | Communication signature | Directional attachment levels (5-level categorical) + presentation descriptions + reasons | Attachment bearings (triggers/blockers) + presentation tendencies (condition→behavior) |
| **Changes** | Slowly (personality-level) | Dynamically (conversation-level) | Slowly (builds from many observations) |
| **Use case** | Personalization, coaching, segmentation | Strategy, prediction, optimization | Deep understanding, influence, adaptation |
